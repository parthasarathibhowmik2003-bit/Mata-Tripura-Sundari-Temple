About the Temple

Discover the spiritual significance of this ancient Shakti Peetha

The Abode of the Universal Mother

Mata Tripura Sundari Temple, also known as Matabari, is one of the 51 Shakti Peethas spread across the Indian subcontinent. Located in the ancient city of Udaipur, the erstwhile capital of Tripura, this temple holds immense religious significance for devotees of Shakti (the Divine Mother).

According to sacred texts, this is the place where the right foot (Sripada) of Goddess Sati fell when Lord Shiva performed the Tandava dance carrying her burnt body. The temple enshrines the sacred idol of Goddess Tripura Sundari, also worshipped as Devi Tripureshwari — the supreme deity of Tripura.

The temple is built in the unique Bengali hut-type architecture with a distinctive square sanctum topped by a conical dome. The main idol, made of reddish-black Kasti stone (also called Chaturdasha Devata), represents the goddess in her benign form with 14 gods and goddesses surrounding her.

Sacred Significance

The temple is considered one of the most important pilgrimage sites in Eastern India. Devotees believe that a visit to this temple washes away sins and grants spiritual liberation. The goddess here is worshipped as the Supreme Power who governs the three worlds — Bhuh (Earth), Bhuvah (Atmosphere), and Svah (Heaven).

Sacred History

Journey through the divine timeline of Mata Tripura Sundari

Mythological Era

The Legend of Shakti Peetha

According to the Devi Bhagavata Purana and Kalika Purana, when Goddess Sati immolated herself in her father Daksha's yajna, Lord Shiva carried her body across the universe. Vishnu's Sudarshan Chakra divided the body into 51 parts, which fell at different places — the Shakti Peethas. The right foot of Sati fell at Udaipur, Tripura.

15th Century

Temple Construction

The present temple structure was built by Maharaja Dhanya Manikya of the Manikya dynasty around 1501 CE. The king, a devout Shakta, established this temple after receiving divine instructions in a dream. The temple became the spiritual center of the Tripura kingdom.

16th-18th Century

Royal Patronage & Expansion

Successive Manikya rulers including Maharaja Ratna Manikya and Maharaja Rama Manikya expanded the temple complex. The Kalyan Sagar (sacred pond) was excavated, and various subsidiary shrines were added. The temple received royal patronage and became the state deity of Tripura.

Present Day

National Heritage & Pilgrimage

Today, the temple is maintained by the Government of Tripura and the Temple Trust. It attracts millions of devotees annually, especially during Diwali (Deepavali Mela) and the Ashwin Navratri. The temple has been recognized as a monument of national importance and continues to be the spiritual heartbeat of Tripura.

Architectural Style

The temple follows the Bengal hut-type (Chala) architectural style with a square base and a conical dome (shikhara). The exterior is painted in sacred red color, symbolizing the divine energy of Shakti. The structure is built without using any iron in its core construction.

The Sacred Idol

The main idol is made of Kasti stone (also called Chaturdasha Devata), a rare reddish-black stone. The goddess is depicted in a benign form with four arms, holding various divine weapons. The idol is surrounded by 14 smaller deities representing the Chaturdasha Devata tradition of Tripura.

Kalyan Sagar

The sacred tank (Kalyan Sagar) covers approximately 6 acres. It is believed that the water has healing properties. The tank is famous for its population of rare soft-shell turtles (Nilssonia nigricans) which are considered sacred and fed by devotees.

Rituals & Festivals

Participate in ancient traditions and vibrant celebrations

Daily Puja & Aarti

The temple follows strict daily rituals starting from Mangala Aarti at 4:00 AM. The day includes multiple puja sessions — Bhoga offering, Sandhya Aarti, and Shayan Aarti. Special Bali (sacrifice) rituals are performed on Tuesdays and Saturdays.

Kumari Puja

During Navratri, the temple performs the sacred Kumari Puja where young girls are worshipped as manifestations of the Divine Mother. This ritual symbolizes the purity and divine feminine energy present in every woman.

Chant & Music

Devotional music using traditional instruments like khol, kartal, and harmonium fills the temple premises. The Shyama Sangeet (devotional songs dedicated to Kali) and bhajans create a spiritually charged atmosphere during evening aartis.

Anna Dana

The temple runs a massive free meal service (Anna Dana) for devotees, especially during festivals. The Maha Prasad is considered highly auspicious and is distributed to thousands of pilgrims daily during major celebrations.

Tantric Practices

Being a Shakti Peetha, the temple preserves ancient Tantric traditions. Special rituals are performed by qualified priests following the Kulachara and Samayachara paths. The temple is one of the few places where authentic Shakta practices continue in their original form.

Havan & Yajna

Regular fire rituals (Havan) are conducted in the temple's yajna-shala. During major festivals, grand Maha Yajnas are performed with thousands of devotees participating. The sacred fire is believed to carry prayers directly to the Divine Mother.

Major Festivals

The temple comes alive during festivals with elaborate decorations, special rituals, and lakhs of devotees seeking blessings. The most significant celebrations are:

Diwali Mela
October/November

7-day grand festival with Deepotsav (lighting of 1 lakh+ diyas)

Navratri
Ashwin Month

Kali Puja
Kartik Amavasya

Grand night-long celebrations with special tantric rituals

Entry & Offerings

  • Entry: Free for all devotees
  • Special Darshan: ₹50-₹200
  • Puja Booking: Available at temple office
  • Prasad: Available from ₹20
  • Photography: Restricted in sanctum
  • Shoe Storage: Free facility available

How to Reach

  • By Air: Maharaja Bir Bikram Airport, Agartala (55 km)
  • By Rail: Udaipur Railway Station (3 km)
  • By Road: Well connected via NH8
  • Local: Auto-rickshaws, taxis available
  • From Agartala: 1.5 hours by road

Accommodation

  • Temple Dharamshala: Free basic stay
  • PWD Bungalow: Government rest house
  • Hotels in Udaipur: ₹800-₹3000/night
  • Agartala Hotels: Wider range available
  • Booking: Advance recommended during festivals

Nearby Facilities

  • Prasad Hall: Free meals during festivals
  • Local Eateries: Bengali & Tripuri cuisine
  • Medical: Udaipur District Hospital nearby
  • ATM: Available within 1 km
  • Shopping: Religious items, local crafts

Visitor Guidelines

  • Dress modestly (traditional attire preferred)
  • Remove shoes before entering temple
  • Maintain silence in the sanctum area
  • Do not touch the main idol
  • Feeding turtles in Kalyan Sagar is allowed with temple-provided food
  • Mobile phones should be on silent mode
